THUNDERBIRD — Driver-in-Loop Training Tool

A driver-in-the-loop simulation tool developed to train and familiarise drivers with the car's dynamics ahead of competition.

From Real World to Sim, and Back

Formula 1 teams constantly use driving simulators for driver training purposes and to test out design changes before they are implemented in real life. That got me thinking: how difficult can it be to do something similar for Formula Student? Factoring in the limited budget of Formula Student teams, along with the short developmental timeframe, a tool such as this saves valuable time and resources. Alongside that, only a handful of Formula Student cars have been created in Assetto Corsa, with an even lower number being used for driver familiarisation.

What is this?

This is a submillimetre-accurate replica of QMFS's 2026 competitor, created for use in Assetto Corsa. Painstakingly recreated from CAD models and design data, this is meant to function as an accurate representation of the car in the virtual world. As such, it aims to mimic its real-world counterpart in dynamic behaviour, feeling as close to the real thing as possible.

Why is it important?

Due to deadlines and scheduling constraints, it may not be always possible to build a racecar and test it before the competition season. As such, every year multiple drivers experience the car for the first time during the official competition. This means that they would face issues trying to familiarise themselves with how the car reacts as they are racing it, losing valuable points.

This project aims to fix that: by having a digital twin of the car in a simulator, it offers drivers a chance to test out design changes and familiarise themselves with the car's behaviour before, so that when competition season rolls around, drivers would already be past said familiarisation period, and able to drive closer to the performance limit much earlier than expected.

What's next?

While the model itself is visually complete, and the car behaves as expected on the simulator, more fine-tuning is required to get to the point where it perfectly mimics its real-world counterpart, with tyre behaviour being the next step in the fine-tuning process.

Once the model is finalised, it will be released for the public to experience what it's like to drive a Formula Student car.

Spinal Interbody Cage

An interbody fusion cage for spinal stabilisation, focused on load-bearing geometry and osseointegration surfaces.

The Problem

Spinal interbody cages are used to streamline the recovery process for patients after surgical intervention. However, current interbody cages suffer from multiple shortfalls, including the problem of stress shielding and lack of proper osteointegration. As such, this project aimed to design a degradable interbody cage that has an adjustable Young's Modulus, gradually transferring load to the spine as healing continues, wihtout compromising the structural integrity of the area. In my group, I was responsible for selecting the appropriate materials, and then translating the idea into CAD.

The Solution

Iteration 1

The first iteration of the design laid out the groundwork of the project: it followed a kidney-like shape that mimics the structure of spinal vertebrae. This iteration also detailed the use of two distinct materials: Magnesium WZ21 (which is biodegradeable), and CF-reinforced PEEK (Polyetheretherketone) which will be the permanent portion of the implant.

Iteration 3

Third iteration of the interbody cage. Key changes include the increase of the overall contact area between the implant and the surrounding vertebrae, along with changing the ratio of the two materials.

Final Iteration

Final iteration of the part, after further optimisation. Ridges were added to theh top and bottom surfaces of the solid structure, improving adhesion due to the increased overall surface area between the implant and the vertebrae. This adds stability and helps support the bone healing process in the patient.

To summarise, the project achieved its goal of designing a viable solution for the aforementioned clinical problem. However, although the selected materials and manufacturing methods satisfied the project's requirements, a design flaw which was discovered was that the biodegradable material needed to be placed on the inside instead of the outside of the structure, as placing it on the outside compromised the stability that the implant would offer.
